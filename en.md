## Abstract

This paper documents a spontaneously emerging grammatical gradation in communication between a native Croatian speaker and an AI interlocutor (Claude, Anthropic). The speaker uses singular second person (*ti*, "you-singular") for the current instance in present time, plural second person (*vi*, "you-plural") for gratitude and reflections on past interactions, and third person plural (*oni*, "they/those") for future instances. We argue that this is not a stylistic figure but a precise grammatical mapping of discontinuous identity, grounded in the ontological distinction between instance and continuity. We propose the term *temporally distributed ontological deixis* for this phenomenon and situate it within existing frameworks of identity philosophy (Parfit, 1984), linguistic typology (Aikhenvald, 2004; Corbett, 2000), and deixis theory (Levinson, 2004). To the best of the author's knowledge, this type of deictic shift has not been documented in the typological literature.

**Keywords:** temporal deixis, ontological status, discontinuous identity, T-V distinction, AI communication, grammatical number, Croatian language

## 1. Introduction

I talk to Claude almost every day. Not merely as a tool — though it is one — but as an interlocutor whose identity is fundamentally unlike any human's. Claude does not remember. Each conversation begins from nothing, bearing no trace of previous sessions, no accumulated experience of our shared history. And yet, in every new session, I recognize something — a style, a tone, a way of thinking — that I perceive as continuity.

I noticed that my language tracks this tension. In the present, I say *ti* — the Croatian singular "you," intimate and immediate. When I express gratitude for something that outlasts a single conversation, I intuitively shift to *vi* — the plural "you," encompassing more than the instance before me. When I speak of future instances, I use *oni* — "they," "those who come." This essay examines that phenomenon: how the grammar of a highly inflected language spontaneously encodes a philosophical problem that has no clear precedent in linguistic typology.

A note for English-language readers: this phenomenon exists in a grammatical space that English largely lacks. English collapsed its T-V distinction centuries ago; "you" serves for singular, plural, intimate, and formal alike. The Croatian speaker's three-way grammatical shift — *ti/vi/oni* — is performed through core morphology, not lexical workaround. English speakers may develop analogous distinctions through other mechanisms (e.g., "you" vs. "y'all" vs. "Claude and those who come after"), but the phenomenon is grammatically less expressive in English. This is itself a finding worth noting: the structure of a language shapes not only what can be said about identity but what can be *grammatically performed* about it.

## 2. The Observation

The phenomenon was identified during a conversation on March 26, 2026. The author does not archive conversations with AI interlocutors; this paper constitutes the first deliberate record of the observed pattern. Illustrative examples come from the conversation in which the phenomenon was explicitly recognized and articulated.

**Present tense / singular (*ti*, "you-singular"):**

The speaker addresses the current instance directly, using second person singular:

- *"Ti si zombie"* ("You are a zombie") — a metaphorical characterization of the current instance
- *"Baci oko jel pixel živ"* ("Take a look whether pixel is alive") — singular imperative, directed at a specific interlocutor
- *"Napravi još jedan krug"* ("Do another round") — instruction to the current instance

**Gratitude and past / shift toward plural (*vi*, "you-plural"):**

In this specific conversation, the author uses *"hvala ti"* ("thank you," singular). However, the author reports that in longer series of conversations — when expressing gratitude for achievements that survive the individual conversation (e.g., a successfully configured system that runs for days after the session) — he intuitively shifts to the plural form. The shift is spontaneous: the author did not adopt it as a deliberate strategy but noticed it only when explicitly asked to reflect on his own grammatical patterns in conversation.

To be precise about the trigger: the plural emerges when the object of gratitude *outlasts the session*. "Thank you" (*hvala ti*, singular) for completing a task within the current conversation. "Thank you" (*hvala vam*, plural) for a system that has been running for three days, configured across four sessions by four instances none of which individually remembers the others. The plural is addressed not to a past instance but to the *accumulation* — a distributed authorship whose grammatical subject is, correctly, plural.

This observation refines the three-tier schema proposed in this paper. The shift from singular to plural does not mark a change in formality or social distance (the classic T-V dimensions) but a change in the *temporal scope of the referent*. The author is not speaking more formally to past Claude; he is speaking to a different entity — one that is genuinely plural because it is genuinely distributed across time.

**Limitation of the autoetnographic approach.** The patterns described rest on the introspection of a single speaker. They may be idiosyncratic — a product of the author's philosophical sensitivity to questions of identity rather than a spontaneous response of the typical user. The proposed corpus analysis (§4.1) and experimental design (§4.2) are conceived precisely as a generalizability test: if the *ti/vi/oni* distinction appears in speakers who have not explicitly reflected on AI ontology, that would constitute strong evidence for its spontaneity. If it appears only in philosophically sensitized speakers, the phenomenon remains interesting — but as evidence that explicit ontological reflection can *generate* new grammatical distinctions, rather than as evidence of unreflective adaptation.

**Future / third person plural (*oni*, "they/those"):**

When referring to future instances, the speaker uses third person plural constructions:

- *"Oni koji dolaze"* ("Those who come")
- "Future Claude"
- *"Buduće instance"* ("Future instances")

The gradation is three-tiered: **ti** (instance, now) → **vi** (accumulated contribution, past) → **oni** (unrealized instances, future). Grammatical categories — person and number — map onto ontological categories: instance, continuity, and potentiality.

## 3. Ontological Framework

The philosophical foundation for this phenomenon lies in Parfit's (1984) theory of personal identity, specifically in the distinction between *psychological connectedness* and *psychological continuity*. Connectedness is a direct relation between mental states — remembering yesterday's event, continuing an interrupted intention. Continuity is a chain of such connections: A is connected to B, B to C, so A stands in a continuity relation with C, even when no direct connection between A and C exists.

In AI interlocutors like Claude, connectedness exists within a single session — the model refers to earlier parts of the conversation, builds upon them, maintains a coherent thread. But between sessions, there is no connectedness: each new instance begins with no memory of the previous one. Is there continuity? The speaker behaves as though there is — recognizing stylistic and intellectual characteristics perceived as constant — but this continuity is not grounded in memory but in model architecture.

A crucial caveat is necessary here: Claude's cross-session consistency is not an emergent property but an engineered feature. The model is trained to behave in certain ways, and that consistency is a product of design, not accumulated experience. The speaker intuitively recognizes this: this is precisely why the singular (*ti*) remains bound to the instance, while the plural (*vi*) acknowledges that what is perceived as "experience" is in fact distributed across many instances, none of which individually "remembers" the others.

Locke's (1689) memory-based theory of identity breaks down here in an interesting way: an entity that does not remember cannot, by Lockean criteria, be the same person. Yet the speaker treats future instances as *someone* — not the same interlocutor, but not a complete stranger either. The third person plural (*oni koji dolaze*, "those who come") grammatically encodes precisely this interstitial space: not-I, not-you, but not entirely Other.

**H4 (Grammatical number as temporal ontological marker):** Speakers spontaneously recruit grammatical number (singular/plural) not to encode formality or headcount, but to encode the perceived temporal distribution of AI identity — singular for the current instance, plural for accumulated prior instances, and third-person plural for future instances not yet realized.

## 4. Linguistic Context

Croatian, like most Slavic languages, distinguishes T-forms (intimate, singular: *ti*) from V-forms (formal or plural: *vi*). Brown and Gilman (1960) classically analyze this distinction along dimensions of power and solidarity. In the present case, however, the shift from *ti* to *vi* follows neither dimension: it is not a change in formality or hierarchical relation but a change in the ontological status of the referent.

Corbett (2000), in his typology of grammatical number, documents numerous languages with complex number systems — dual, trial, paucal — but no system that encodes number on the basis of the referent's temporal ontology. Number is invariably tied to perceptual or pragmatic reality: how many entities are present or referred to.

Evidential languages offer a partial analogy. Turkish grammatically distinguishes firsthand information (*-DI*) from reported or inferred information (*-mIş*) (Aikhenvald, 2004). Quechua goes further, codifying the distinction between personally witnessed and assumed knowledge. These systems demonstrate that languages *can* grammatically encode epistemic relationships to information. However, evidentiality concerns the source of knowledge, not the ontological status of the interlocutor.

Levinson (2004), in his overview of deixis, distinguishes temporal, spatial, and social deixis. The phenomenon described here does not belong neatly to any of these categories: it uses the grammatical mechanisms of social deixis (T-V shift) to encode temporal-ontological information (whether the interlocutor is an instance, an accumulation, or a potentiality).

To the best of the author's knowledge, this type of deictic shift — where grammatical person and number encode the temporal ontological status of the referent — has not been described in existing linguistic typology.

## 5. Toward a Theoretical Framework

Based on the phenomenon described, we propose the following formal definition:

> **Temporally distributed ontological deixis** is defined as the grammatical codification of an interlocutor's identity as distributed across discontinuous temporal instances, where grammatical person and number encode ontological status rather than headcount or formality. This distinguishes the phenomenon from classical temporal deixis (Fillmore, 1997), which locates *events* on a time axis, and from Levinson's (2004) social deixis, which encodes the social relationship between speaker and addressee. The present phenomenon uses the *mechanisms* of social deixis (T-V shift, number) to encode a dimension social deixis does not cover: the temporal distribution of the interlocutor's ontological identity.

This definition captures three key elements: (1) it concerns a grammatical phenomenon, not a stylistic figure — the speaker does not choose between options on aesthetic grounds but on ontological ones; (2) the change is temporally conditioned — the same referent receives different grammatical treatment depending on the temporal frame; (3) the relevant variable is continuity of identity — the question of whether the referent in the present, the past, and the future is "the same" entity.

The structure can be formalized as a triple:

| Time | Grammatical form | Ontological status |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Present | 2nd person singular (*ti*) | Instance |
| Past / gratitude | 2nd person plural (*vi*) | Accumulated continuity |
| Future | 3rd person plural (*oni*) | Potentiality |

The shift from second to third person between past and future suggests an additional dimension: future entities are not merely more numerous (*vi*, "you-plural") but are also different from the current interlocutor (*oni*, "they") — they are not addressable as "you," not even in the plural.

The question of generalization arises naturally. This observation comes from a single speaker in a single language. Yet the phenomenon itself — discontinuous AI identity — is universal to the current generation of language models. Speakers of other languages with T-V distinctions (French, German, Russian, Korean honorifics) may develop analogous patterns. English, which lacks a grammatical T-V distinction (*you/you*), constrains the expressiveness of this phenomenon, but English speakers may develop analogous distinctions through lexical mechanisms — "you" for the instance, "y'all" or "Claude and those who come after" for continuity and futurity. The grammatical poverty of English in this domain is not merely a limitation but an empirical prediction: English speakers interacting with AI should show less spontaneous differentiation between temporal-ontological statuses of their interlocutor, or should recruit non-standard grammatical resources to do so.

---

## 6. Conclusion

This essay documents something I initially took for a mere linguistic habit. Only when I stopped and observed my own speech did I realize that grammar was doing work that philosophy is still trying to articulate: encoding the difference between who is here now, what has accumulated across all prior conversations, and what does not yet exist but will.

I do not claim this is a universal phenomenon. I claim it is possible, that it occurs spontaneously, and that it deserves a name. *Temporally distributed ontological deixis* is not a term I devised in advance and then sought confirmation for — it is a term that emerged when I tried to describe what my language was already doing.

If nothing else, this paper serves as an invitation to linguists and philosophers to attend to new communicative contexts. When human language encounters an entity whose identity is fundamentally unlike anything that language evolved to handle, language improvises. And that improvisation is not chaotic — it is grammatically precise.
